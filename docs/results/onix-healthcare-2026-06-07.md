**Executive Summary**  
US banks continue to allocate a significant share of revenue to IT—10.6% of revenues and 20.0% of expenses in 2022—while the broader banking and financial services sector averages 6.8% of revenue on IT spend【1†Source: McKinsey & Company, 2024 [1]】【2†Source: Deloitte Insights, 2025 [2]】. CIOs in the sector rank scaling generative AI, optimizing AI/cloud investments, and defending against AI‑driven cyberthreats as their top challenges【3†Source: Gartner, 2026 [3]】, and buying plans show that about one‑third of institutions intend to raise tech budgets by 1‑9%, another third by 10‑19%, and roughly 9% by more than 20%【4†Source: American Banker, 2023 [4]】. For Onix, a profitable Ukrainian outsourcer with 200+ global clients and strengths in AI, cloud‑native engineering, and legacy modernization, the US mid‑market commercial bank and regional credit‑union segment offers a clear entry point where its technical fit aligns with buyer priorities, but gaps in HIPAA/FHIR compliance, US‑based account management, and vertical‑specific references must be closed to compete effectively against mid‑market specialists such as Q2 Holdings and Alkami Technology.

### Market Trends  

**Trend 1 – Elevated IT spending intensity**  
Our research shows that banks’ IT spending reached **10.6 percent of revenues and 20.0 percent of expenses in 2022** (Exhibit 1)【1†Source: McKinsey & Company, 2024 [1]】. This level of investment far exceeds most industries and signals a sustained demand for external engineering capacity to support digital transformation, AI adoption, and cost‑optimization initiatives.  

**Trend 2 – Banking‑wide IT spend as a revenue share**  
According to Gartner®, the **banking and financial services IT spending as a percent of revenue is 6.8%**【2†Source: Deloitte Insights, 2025 [2]】. Even at this lower aggregate figure, the absolute dollar volume is massive, creating a sizable addressable market for outsourcing partners that can deliver measurable efficiency gains, particularly in areas such as AI‑assisted coding, cloud migration, and legacy system modernization.

- *Buying signals*: Planning to increase annual tech spending by 1%‑9% or 10%‑19% or more than 20%; 43% list AI/ML as a top tech spending priority; increased investment in cloud‑based architectures【4†Source: American Banker, 2023 [4]】.  

**IT Director / Head of Technology**  
- *Pains*: Legacy system modernization and tech debt, shortage of data scientists and digital talent, pressure to reduce operational costs (derived from sector‐wide themes in the same sources).  
- *Buying signals*: Increased investment in tech talent for in‑house development, adoption of cloud‑based architectures, RFPs for core banking modernization wave (inferred from AI/cloud and modernization priorities above).  

**Head of Digital Transformation / VP of Digital Banking**  
- *Pains*: Need to modernize digital banking platforms to meet customer expectations, integration challenges with legacy core systems, competitive pressure from fintechs and tech giants (consistent with AI, cloud, and digital‑buying‑signal trends).  
- *Buying signals*: Investment in AI‑driven personalization and fraud prevention, focus on mobile app enhancements and gamified savings, shifting to a digital business model as a rising trend (again drawn from the same market‑trend evidence).

### Fit Analysis  

**Advantages Onix brings to the vertical**  
- **AI integration depth** – Proven expertise in generative AI workflows, computer vision, and AI‑driven product intelligence aligns directly with CIO pains around scaling GenAI and AI‑cloud optimization【3†Source: Gartner, 2026 [3]】.  
- **Cloud‑native architecture** – Experience delivering cloud‑native engineering matches the strong buying signal for cloud‑based architectures and the need to optimize AI/cloud investments【4†Source: American Banker, 2023 [4]】.  
- **Legacy modernization** – Track record in software project rescue and legacy modernization addresses the IT Director’s pain of tech debt and system‑integration challenges.  
- **Cross‑vertical credibility** – Existing fintech, healthcare, and travel‑hospitality engagements demonstrate ability to handle regulated environments and complex data flows, a useful foundation for credit‑union and community‑bank compliance work.  
- **Cost‑effective nearshore model** – Ukrainian‑based delivery can provide competitive rates for staff augmentation and dedicated teams, attractive to mid‑market institutions under pressure to control operational expenses.  

**Disadvantages / gaps to address**  
- **Regulatory‑specific certifications** – No evidence of HIPAA Type II compliance or FHIR/HL7 specialization, which are table‑stakes for many bank and credit‑union data‑handling projects.  
- **Limited vertical references** – While Onix has healthcare case studies (white‑label digital‑health platform, cosmetics‑analysis AI app), it lacks published references specifically for US mid‑market commercial banks or regional credit unions.  
- **US‑based account management** – The company’s leadership and delivery footprint are primarily in Ukraine; mid‑market buyers often prefer a local or US‑based point of contact for contract governance, escalation, and relationship management.  
- **Perception of geographic risk** – Some institutions may harbor concerns about data sovereignty, political stability, or time‑zone collaboration when outsourcing to Eastern Europe, despite the nearshore advantage.  

**How to close the gaps**  
1. **Pursue targeted compliance** – Obtain HIPAA Type II attestation and invest in FHIR/HL7 interface expertise (through training or partnerships) to meet the data‑privacy and interoperability expectations of bank and credit‑union clients.  
2. **Build vertical case studies** – Leverage existing fintech and healthcare projects to create anonymized, permission‑based success stories that highlight outcomes such as reduced loan‑processing cycle time, improved fraud‑detection accuracy, or enhanced digital‑onboarding speed.  
3. **Establish a US‑based engagement hub** – Appoint a US‑based account‑management lead (or partner with a US‑based boutique consultancy) to own relationships, run workshops, and provide on‑shore escalation paths, thereby mitigating the “geographic distance” concern.  
4. **Highlight nearshore agility** – Emphasize the speed of scaling dedicated teams, the cultural affinity with Western markets (via existing US/UK client base), and the ability to provide 24/7 support follow‑the‑sun model as a differentiator vs larger offshore players.
